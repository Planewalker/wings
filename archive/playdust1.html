Part One

I was sitting in a Sigilian bar, sipping at a shot of liquid gold. It was just another day in the Cage, just another rainy day, to be precise. And anyone who knows the Cage knows that the rain there is thick as oil. In fact, some have speculated that it really is oil. I'd been caught out in it, ran to the nearest tavern, and, as there was nothing else to do, proceeded to get drunk. By the time he walked in, I was about halfway there. He was a drow, tall for one, dressed in black, as they usually are, double swords hanging from his waist. I hear that on the Prime, a drow's appearance will cause panic and dozens of people trying to take off said drow's head from said drow's neck. In the Cage, we get everything. Creatures of all kinds, and one drow is not going to cause a row unless he actually tries to provoke it, and even then, we'd jump on him because he was trying to provoke a row, not because of his race. Anyway, the drow didn't order anything. Instead, he walked over to a table where a middle aged tiefling, probably a mage, I figured from his garments, sat. The tiefling had apparently already ordered for the both of them, as there were two wine cups sitting there on that table. I noticed, though, that the drow never actually drank any of the wine. I watched with idle interest as a conversation commenced, not that I could hear any of it, but the drow was really good looking, and even the tiefling wasn't half bad. Okay, so I wasn't strictly watching. I was leering, quietly, and from a distance. The drow seemed to be telling a story, as he talked on a length, with the tiefling occasional sticking in questions or some such. Afterwards, they both leaned back in contemplation, thoughtful looks on their faces. A few minutes after, the tiefling said his goodbyes, and walked out. I shrugged and went back to contemplating the drow, who was staring up at the ceiling.

Suddenly, he drew a dagger from his cloak, stared at it for a few minutes. So, I'm sitting there, wondering just what he's going to do with the thing, wondering why I had to get drunk today. If I'd been a bit more coherent, I would have went over and introduced myself. But-no such luck. The drow sighed, stuck the dagger back into his cloak, and stood up. As he walked out the door, I sighed too, in disappointment. As the last bit of that sigh left my mouth, my table suddenly shuddered a bit. Dragging my eyes over the source of the disturbance, I came face to face with a sometime employer of mine, an overgrown scro-based vampire type. He's one of the Dustmen, go figure. Not everyone is part of a faction, of course, but Sigil has it's fair share of faction-types. He grinned at me, real suggestive-like, smug as hell. Ya, right, buddy, I'm thinking. What in Abyss are you here for? Out loud, I just acknowledge his presence, saying his name grudgingly.

"Kyger," I sounded surly.

He grinned again, this time at my less than welcoming tone. Anyone that knows me, knows that conversation, when I'm drunk, is not my long-suit.

"Tirysa," he spoke my name in return.

We sat there, staring at each other for several minutes, waiting to see which one of us would break the silence first. I despise games of any sort, especially these "greater will" ones, so when someone plays them, I usually ignore it, or take violent measures to end it, each course of action depending upon my state of mind and/or body at the time. In this instance, I took option A, most due to my state of body.

"I have a job for you," he finally spoke.

Well, now we're getting somewhere.

"Oh?"

He nodded, smiled, elaborated. "As you know, my representative faction," and here I take a pause a mental snort of disdain. Representative faction? Ya, right. ". . .Is interested in the dead, and the undead, of all types. It has recently come to our attention that there has been in Sigil, upon occasion, an unknown type of undead creature. We would like you to locate it, currently, and extract skin, and if it still carries the substance, blood samples, so that the Dustmen, as we are so crassly referred to, may deduce the circumstances of this creature's undead making."

Talk about long winded, why don't you? Scro are rare in the Planes, as they tend to stick in the Prime's Wildspace, but those Planars that know scro know that they tend to be far more intelligent than their cousins, the orcs. Let me tell you, it just makes them ten times more arrogant. Kyger may have the jink to hire me, but he himself is nothing more than an overblown egotist.

"Will you except it?" he asked.

"Fine," I responded, before I actually had time to really think it over. High priced merc or not, in order to obtain that high-price you've got to have cutters willing to hire you, and that hadn't been happening much lately. Things had been quiet, or at least as quiet as Sigil gets.

"Good," he smiled.

"So, just who is this new gobbly-gook?" I snickered.

He ignored my commentary there, knowing full well what I personally thought of undead. It had never stopped him from hiring me, and had never stopped me from excepting.

"A drow. The name is Zaknafein Do'Urden. A warrior, by all reports. A very skilled one. You'll have to be very quick, and very quiet to complete this task," he smiled, tit for tat like.

Then, he reached into his cloak, pulling out a flat disk adorned with a single small switch, of the like that spell-casters use to capture images within. The scro pressed the switch, and an image sprang upon from the disk. It was of a drow, tall for one, dressed in black, twin long swords about his waist, and very good looking. The same drow from the very bar I was sitting in.

"This is the subject," Kyger said.

Well, maybe I should re-evaluate my opinion of undead, I thought. At least. . .some of them.

Part Two

"So," I drawled into the face of a long time friend of mine, a slight grin tracing the outline of my lips, "Do you know of him?"

Now sober, I was able to deal at this situation with more clarity. Certainly, it was an intriguing "assignment," and I had every intention of going through on this. But on the next day, details were began to surface in my mind. Nothing was ever so simple as finding someone and getting a job done.

My friend, a female gnome called Meg, looked at me dubiously, and questioned, "Why are asking me about it? Do I look like an information broker?" And she wasn't, technically. Still, Meg knew things, knew what was happening around her, knew how to come through for her friends.

"I just accepted a job," I began to explain, "But things have been down so low lately that the only jink I've got has to go to my rent, see? So, I figured, you may have heard something. My employer said the cutter's been seen around the Cage, so. . ." I trailed off suggestively.

She eyed me irritably for a long moment. It's true, I don't have the most stable income. Most mercs don't. Then again, most save up as they go along. I don't, and I never have. Any jink I get burns a hole in my pocket. Not literally, but it weights upon my mind until I just have to buy something. So, by the time it comes to purchase something I need, I usually have nothing left. Meg was used to this, used to giving information to me, which I did pay for, eventually. But still, she never ceased to give me a hard time about it when I asked. She probably meant for me to change my ways, but I don't see than happening any time soon.

"Tirysa, I don't know how you've survived all these years, depending upon others as you do. You've got no responsibility, and the only reason you ever managed to leech as much jink as you do, out of those jobs you get, is 'cause you target idiots to hire you," she lectured, eyeing me sternly.

We both knew though, that the speech was just for show. True, some of my employers are thick-skulled, mush-brained bashers, but that's just the pick of the draw, so to speak. Irresponsible I may be, but I do know how to fight, and how to be subtle, when I need to be. I know how to earn my pay.

"I swear," I began to promise, bargaining, "When I get my full pay, I'll give you one third," I promised, being highly generous. I usually was, in these promises. The amount would go down, later, I knew, and so did she.

"Full pay?" she inquired archly.

"Well, Kyger did give me some-"

"Kyger? You accepted another job from him?" she grumbled. She'd never liked Kyger, and though I wasn't too fond of him either, her dislike was more intense than mine ever was.

"I was drunk," I defended myself, "And besides, it sounded interesting."

Looked good too, I added to myself, silently, but since I had only found out who the subject was after I accepted the job, it didn't really count as a reason.

"That's no excuse, Tir. None at all," she paused a moment, muttered under her breath and, finally, added, "But I know what kind of trouble you could get in if you don't get a job from the Dustmen finished. So, yes, I have heard of the drow. He's a loner, keeps to himself. Well, he does know one cutter in the Cage. A tiefling mage called Grew. He owns a mageshop. The tiefling, that is. Grew's the only one that the drow seems to socialize with," she shrugged, and added, "All I know besides that is that he hires himself out a merc, to very select customers. Not necessarily rich ones, though most are, but he seems to have a code."

"A bit more than you would usually would know, off hand," I pointed out, a bit suspicious.

She shrugged, and eyed me irritably. "Someone else was asking about him, somewhere, and I happened to overhear them," she trailed off, obviously not want to say anymore.

Just overheard someone? Coincidence, or what? Who else was asking about this drow? And another mercenary? Interesting. Not that mercenaries are uncommon, or anything. I mean, any berk can tell you they're not. But I wondered why Kyger hadn't told me that. 'The plot thickens, as it were,' I thought to myself with a grin. Far from being put off, I was beginning to truly enjoy myself.

"Interesting," I drawled to Meg, and then, with a grin, and a wink at the gnome, I yelled "Thanks!" as I skipped out the door, before even she could get a word in edgewise.

Part Three

It was dark and dirty, and not in a good way. That was the area of town that Meg had chosen to live in, and who was I to protest? Well, I did. I did almost every time I didn't want something from her. I get in my jabs when I have nothing to loose and something to gain, upon general principle. I hoped that this Grew's mageshop wasn't in this area. They generally aren't, mageshops, but you never know. I should have asked Meg for directions, but that would have given her the chance to further pick my brain and job, which just is not a comfortable position to be in. And, after all, I snickered sarcastically to myself as I stalked up the street, remaining to question further would have ruined my theatrical exit. It was too late, though. If I went back, I would get nothing from Meg now, and I knew it. One strike and you're out is pretty much that gnome's life-motto.

Sigil is confusing place to live, huge and multi-dimensional. Almost no one bothers to keep track of everything in it. But someone, somewhere, knows a bit of it, and some other someone knows another bit. It all adds up. There was someone who could tell me where the tiefling's mageshop was. But, that would have to wait. I now knew for sure now that someone was following me. They were being subtle, footsteps light, shadows concealed, but I could feel it. I could sense the eyes upon my back, the anticipation. It was building in me, as well. The lust for violence, the shock when the one *hunting* me found out just who was the hunter. The other, a shadow at my back, was much closer now. I let him approach, wanting him near me, every particle of my being now focused upon the situation at hand.

Without warning, I whirled upon my pursuer, smashing it up against a wall with all my might, slapping it's face several times before I even viewed it, and pushing my own snarling visage right in.

"Do you know where the mageshop run by a tiefling named Grew is?" I growled, demanding an answer.

My captive gave a slow blink. He certainly hadn't been expecting that question.

The mageshop was dark inside, but not shabbily so. It was an elegant, and, truth to tell, expensive sort of darkness. The walls were cool grey marble, as were the shelves upon which the wares were piled. Cabinets full of magical trinkets, bags of components, bits of this and that. I don't know magic well, but I know quality. The very air smelt rich. Obviously, this was no bargain basement shop.

Lounging in a chair of silver leather was the self-same tiefling I had spotted in the tavern but a day before. He was reading from a large, purple skinned tome. Most people would assume it was a spellbook, but I would point out that wizards need to have their fun, too, and guess it was a trashy novel.

Though most, in this sort of situation, would take a round about approach, asking sly questions and such like, I, as I have mentioned upon previous occasion, have never had the patience for such games. I walked up to the wizard, steps purposeful, and without pretense I asked,

"Do you know of the current whereabouts of Zaknafein Do'Urden?"

"Why do you want to know?" questioned a voice, smooth and accented and most certainly not emerging from the tiefling, who was looking up at me from his leather perch like I was a complete and utter moron.

I turned toward the door, where the voice had directed itself from. Standing there was the self-same "mystery undead" I had been hired to acquire samples of. He was eyeing me with a mix of suspicion and contemptuous amusement. The tiefling gave a quiet chuckle, and went back to his book, tuning out my existence. Have you ever felt as though you were the butt of some unknown joke, which becomes all the more un-amusing for you because you don't know 'why' everyone is laughing at you? That is just the way I felt at the very moment the tiefling gave his chuckle.

The drow did not wait for a reply, he simply began walking toward me, unhurriedly, smiling ever so slightly. I shrugged at him, trying to appear as unhurried and casual as he seemed.

"A friend of mine is interested in your services," I replied lightly, mixing the truth with lies, as it were.

"Maybe it's that idiot from Limbo you told me about," the tiefling suggested, not looking up from his gaudily-covered book.

"Certainly not," the drow spoke, "It would have simply whirled in here itself, rather than extending transparent offers of employment through suspicious looking friends."

I almost replied that I did not look suspicious, but the drow was looking more unfriendly by the minute. So, I thought to myself, how are I going to get this job done when my target is supposedly a whole lot more skilled than I am, and is already looking at me like I'm dinner? Okay, maybe that was an incorrect metaphor. I mean, it's not as though he's a vampire.

"I'm serious," I replied as though I were hurt by his mistrust.

"No doubt," was his wry reply as he stretched out, like a cat, in one of the empty silver leather chairs. I stood there, as though nothing were out the ordinary as he fixed me with piercing eyes. Suddenly, I was struck by an idea.

"My friend's name is Kyger. He's heard about you, and wants to hire you for a job. He's been very busy, lately, though, and so he sent me. I extend his invitations of hospitality to you, in good faith," I smiled after delivering this impromptu mini-speech.

The drow's slender white eyebrows lifted up high now, in genuine amusement. My plan, which was meant to accomplish both of my goals, was now set into motion. Now, the only thing to do was wait and see if my target would take the bait. If he excepted my "invitations of hospitality" I would most certainly do my best to make his comfortable. And then, I would bind him and take the samples desired by the Dustmen. Job accomplished. Most people would say that this being seemed a bit too knowing to fall for such tricks, but old methods often work best.

I smiled again, not innocently, as that would have been a dead give away, but professionally, not giving away a thought in my head.

Part Four

I gave a small smile as I poured the wine into the goblets on the table, wondering just what his reaction would be. I don't know, some undead can drink stuff, but others couldn't even if their unlives depended upon it. This one, though, simply gave a small grin and ignored the cup. I was, I'll admit, disappointed. I was looking for something of a more extreme reaction. But then, what the hell? There isn't much you can say to a cup of wine, unless it's some sort of secret weapon against your breed or some such. I sat down across from him, letting the silence increase it's own weight as the time went on. He, however, seemed perfectly content to let the room maintain it's silence. When this drow had accepted my invitation, I was truly gleeful, and yet. . .There was something very knowing, very canny about Zaknafein. I received the feeling that he was perfectly aware of my plans. All there was left was whether he was going to go along with them. There was another thing, though, that I had neglected to truly contemplate. He was undead, and quite a few undead types just don't work in that manner. I frowned to myself, deeply. What a perfectly horrid thought. Well, best to dive in head first and find out, rather than never know.

I leaned forward, and my spider-silk tunic dipped down to outline the curves of my body. Rest assured, I know how to dress for occasion. A spark of knowing interest lite in the drow's eyes as his long hands ran down the velvet covered arms of the chair he was seated in. I smiled.

My first thought upon waking was that my unasked question had been answered. He most certainly worked. My second thought, chasing at lightening speed after the first, was that I was once more dressed, with the sudden addition of being bound by adamantium cables, which molded to my wrists and ankles with slithery snakes, and yet were as unmovable as age old boulders. Judging from the grim expression upon the face of the drow who was, once more, sitting in the velvet chair, whilst I was bound, sitting on the chair opposite him, this was not some sort of kinky game.

"Untie me at once!" I shouted before I quite took the time to think, voice sounding imperiously enough. I think I've been spending too much time around self-righteous scro.

"I don't think so," his voice was no longer half edged in amusement. It was flat and cold. He had me where he wanted me, and there was no backing out now. It seemed that he had turned my plan upon itself, and aimed it right in my own direction. I really hate it when that happens.

"You have to give the Dustmen credit," he spoke again, "They do learn after a some near a dozen agents leave my company a bit more dead than they entered it. I suppose they hired you in hopes I would not suspect such a fool as their agent."

Huh? Okay, I was somewhat insulted by the fool bit, but the fact that there have been Dustmen sent after this one. . .Well, let's just say that I'd be having an interesting conversation with Kyger when I got out of this situation. If I got out of this situation.

I scowled at him and snarled, "What is all this for!? I have no idea what you're talking about!"

He eyed me with the exaggerated patience of one who had hear such things before and had absolutely no belief in protests of the sort. Personally, I really couldn't blame him. I wouldn't believe it either, but perhaps if I kept it up long enough, he would start to doubt that he was correct, which would give me the time to at least attempt to figure an escape.

"I have every idea what I am talking about. You are not the one, after all, who was hunted for samples of your body tissues, but which to control you," he hissed, sudden anger lighting his eyes in a flash of crimson fire. It is just not pleasant to be on the receiving end of such look from those eyes. Wait a moment, I thought to myself as his words sunk in, to control you? Well, I can see why you would be pissed off. Not that I'm surprised that the Dusties would want to control a new undead type, but I was lead to believe my little collection job was simply for research purposes. "It was, actually," I snickered to myself, "With a vengeance."

"Who knows," I smirked at him then, "You may enjoy it. Then again, the Dustmen never did have much imagination," I laughed.

Contrary to the annoyance I had expected, the knowing rage that flashed with brief, blatant appearance over his features was shocking. Apparently, I had hit a nerve.

"I assure you," he growled, "I know very well what being controlled is like, and I do not enjoy it. I do not care to experience anything the Dustmen have in store for me. I do care to keep my will to myself. To that end, you are going to tell me exactly how you came to be hunting for me."

I sighed, deep, long, and most importantly to him, resignedly. The way I saw it, at this point, I had no choice to speak of. I opened my mouth, and began to recount the events of the last couple days. With certain exceptions to the events, of course.

He was somewhat glum and annoyed by the time I was finished my narrative. Apparently, I knew nothing useful. However, a few, silent moments later and he stood, walking over to stand beside me. He drew the dagger he had drawn in the tavern not even two days before and retrieved from my waist a loose-tied pouch I kept there. Without a warning, dagger moving faster than my eyes could follow it, the dagger neatly sliced off a long sliver of flesh from his forearm. I grimaced in shock, and he grimaced in distaste as he dropped the flesh into the pouch.

"What's that for!?"

"You," he explained, "Will take this to your employer. Act as though you acquired it without my assistance. Thus, he will analyse the powers and limitations of my current state of being. The study will also reveal how the Dustmen would go about controlling my form. If I have this information, I can almost certainly prevent it. If you deliver this without incident, you will be fine. However, if you deviate from that course, I will kill you," he spoke with absolute seriousness, but I simply could not resist a jab. It revealed my nervousness, actually. I generally become more sarcastic as I become more upset.

"Ah, and after all we've shared," I mocked.

The by now familiar contemptuous look of amusement flashed in his eyes for the briefest of moments before he spoke, tone just as mocking as my own had been.

"All's fair in love and war."

Part Five

I forced a purposeful edge to my stride, the confident feel of one who has well completed a job showing in the fall of my boots, but inside, I was very annoyed. I mean, there's only so much a girl can put up with in a few days. Having your own plans turned on you, is rather like having your nose rubbed in your own dung. Still, what was I to do? The quickest way to escape from the situation was to deliver the skin. If the drow wanted to risk it, that was his problem. I was just going to hand the sample over the Kyger, and get out Sigil for a while, at least until Zaknafein Do'Urden was out of the city. I think he meant what he said. Of course, threats of death can be simply bluff, upon the part of some beings, but somehow, I don't think that drow is the bluffing type.

Kyger's "domicile" was a richly appointed house carved from black marble. Funny how the Dustmen worship death, and yet their possessions seem so free of decay. Of course, I am sarcastic. All the factions tend to hypocritical in some manner or another. I knocked upon the heavy door three times in a row, playing up to a joke that Kyger wouldn't appreciate. They say Death knocks three times before he comes for you, or some such. But by this time, I didn't care if Kyger didn't appreciate my humours. Just as I was about to knock again, the door was opened, and a thin, fleshy face peeked through. A zombie, I noted as I grimaced. Most Dustmen of a certain rank have a few token undead or so. Judging from the lowliness of zombies in general though, Kyger wasn't too high on the scale. The zombie moved an arm jerkily, motioning me to enter the house. Cautiously, keeping the zombie in view, I stepped inside the door. The zombie looked me over, and I could have sworn he was taking my measure. After a moment, it pointed to a polished corner table in the hallway. Giving the zombie a dirty look as I did, I walked to the table. There was a note upon it.

Tirysa,

I am so sorry, my dear, but I have urgent and sudden out of Sigil business at this time that simply cannot be ignored. Assuming you are reading this, I am still away and you have completed your task. Thus, I advice you to take the samples to a trusted colleague of mine, Freqtw, at his residence in Sigil. He can be found but several houses from my own domicile. It's the green one with the yellow trimming. Genius he may be, but that wizard has no taste. He knows who you are, and I'm sure he'll be very helpful. Have a good day.

Regards,

Kyger

That pompous bastard! was my first thought after I placed the letter back upon the table. How dare he send me to some wizard who no doubt has no instructions or desire to pay me. Damn it. I really would be having a talk with Kyger, I swore once more to myself. Best to get this over with, though, I reminded myself. Grimacing I prepared to face the horror of the yellow and green mansion.

If there is one thing that Kyger and I agree upon, it is just how ugly that house really is.

I could not help myself from giving into the urge to theatrically shudder as I rang the huge and gaudy golden bell that hung above the enormous double doors of the house. I was halfway between the twin urges to hurl and to laugh. A moment later and the door opened with cautious force, rendering both options to the background of my thoughts. A human man, wearing silver-gray robes that matched his wild gray hair, which stuck out in every direction possible, and then some. His eyes were a darker, steely gray that were filled with caution and wild inspiration.

"Who are you?" he asked, irritably enough.

I stuck my hand on my hip, pursed my lips and announced, "I am Tirysa."

The wizard, Freqtw, stared at me for a long moment, and then he shrugged. "Oh, well. Are you here with that skin stuff?"

"Uh, yes," I said, staring at him somewhat incredulously.

"Well, come on in then. But don't expect me to pay you anything!"

I gave a groan as I stepped in the door, thinking of that comment, 'Just as I suspected.'

The inside of the wizard's house was somewhat shocking. I could not help but gape as I gazed up the exquisite decoration, subtle highlights of metallics and pearl inlaid through the main hall and the visible adjoining rooms. Artifacts and stark furniture adorned here and there, the whole impression amounting to dark, barren beauty. I hadn't realized that my mouth was slightly hanging open until the wizard reached over, grasped my chin, and clamped my jaw shut. I yanked away from his touch, and opened my mouth again to ask, but he beat me to the question.

"The outside keeps the thieves away. They assume that the inside is just as bad," he grinned, evidently quite pleased with his reasoning. I have to admit, though, that it was rather clever.

"Oh. I suppose you get that question a lot, hmm?"

"Actually, no. Most people that come in here are beyond asking," he grinned meaningfully.

"Wonderful," I said.

I was steadily following him as we walked, down the halls. By the time the last comment exited my mouth, we turned into a room, plush and full over a multitude of velvet cushions. On a long mahogany table, there rested several huge books, and crystal vases and bottles of components. Once more, I turned incredulously to the wizard.

"Is this your laboratory?"

"Yup," he confirmed. Noticing my look, he scowled, "What? Did you expect me to conform to every stereotype about the dusty wizard laboratories, skulls on shelves, and cobwebs in corners?"

"Of course not," I replied, thinking, 'Whoa, this guy is a fruitcake.'

That was probably one of those stereotypes the wizard was thinking about, but hey, no one is perfect.

"Anyway," the wizard said as he lounged in a pile of green cushions, "Give me the skin."

"Sure," I snapped. "Tell Kyger he owns me my pay, though, and don't forget it."

I heard the wizard snort in laughter as I shoved the leather pouch containing Zaknafein's skin sample towards him, just before I wheeled away, and stormed down the hall, slamming the door as I exited the house.

Part Six

I walked into the apartment I call home when in Sigil. It was appointed tastefully, walls painted midnight blue, with rich black leather furniture all about. Reminders of profitable times is what this apartment was. 'I will not be seeing these rooms for quite sometime,' I thought to myself. I would take out that old extra dimension pack, stuff it full of clothes and other possessions, and then get out of town. It was a good plan for one who did not want to get any more caught up in messy situations than she already has. There was only one complication, and that was acquiring my pay from Kyger once he got back. I decided, however, to simply contact him via a wizard after several months. By that time, he should have returned to Sigil. Complication unravelled, I set out to find my magic bag.

It was in the old steel trunk in my bedroom, laying nicely with the scant other magic items I had managed to acquire over the years. There was ring of healing, which wouldn't fix you if you got stabbed in the heart, but would close up a gash in your arm or some such. There was a that singing dagger, which was just a miniature version of a singing sword. And then there was the bag. It was a midnight blue that, incidentally, matched my walls, and it had the stereotypical moons and stars all over it. But it was very useful, and so I put up with the little pictures. I pulled it out, and stuffed the ring inside, as well as strapping the dagger on my person. I also included the pile of ready jink I kept in the trunk. I then proceeded to pack clothing. It took me roughly an hour to ready myself, but finally, I was finished. Shrugging on a black cloak, I walked out of my apartment, locking the door firmly behind me. I decided, though, to let my gnome friend, Meg, know that I would be gone for a while. She deserved to at least know that much, and the extra hour or so it would take wouldn't hurt me, I thought.

That was where I was wrong.

"I'll be leaving the city for a while," I announced to her without preamble.

Her little face clouded upon with decidedly not a little amount of displeasure. The scowl seemed to vibrate in the air. I coughed. Meg angry is not a pretty sight, but I was seeing it.

"What?" she snarled. "What about my pay?"

I just knew she was going to say that. I mean, I would be saying it if I were her. In fact, I'm me, and I've been wondering about that myself. Pretty much all my friends and acquaintances are very well and self acquainted with that very phrase.

"That's why I'm letting you know I'll be gone, Meg. I just didn't want you to be wondering. But I swear, I will give it to you as soon as--"

"As soon as possible," she interrupted sarcastically.

"Exactly!"

"Right," she snorted, "So, what is the great and urgent matter that takes you out of city in such haste?"

"Remember that job that I asked for your help about? The one that Kyger hired me to do? Well, it sort of backfired."

"Sort of backfired? And this was a surprise? I told you before, don't get involved with that vampire. He's nothing but trouble."

"He's just an occasional employer," I muttered sourly, "It's not as though he's courting me."

"Not that he wouldn't enjoy that. Or at least the quick and easy meaning he would tack onto that ritual," she retorted.

"Of course I know that. Every planar knows that. I don't have any such spells memorized, however. I work more with the dead, than the living."

"You are a necromancer?" asked the other drow wizard.

"Yes," Freqtw answered, somewhat haughtily, and then he deflated as he scowled again. "Though lately I've been wishing I'd chosen another vocation. That way, I wouldn't have gotten into this situation to begin with."

The Sorcery elf lifted an eyebrow in a "do-tell" sort of gesture. Zaknafein had his hood down, so his face couldn't be seen, but his posture said that he wanted to strangle the Dustman.

"A deal I made with a colleague," Freqtw explained shortly, displaying some thought, and not really explaining at all. Luckily for him.

At that moment, we emerged into a cavern of sorts, in which the faint glowing of the stones, the colourful mosses and lichens, and entire off feeling of the Underdark was missing.

"This chamber was prepared by several wizards who wished to use magic to travel without the potential side effects," Jalynfein explained. "There are several matching chambers in various places about the Underdark. One of the those is in Skullport, where my travel stone is. I have that in because it also stabilizes the process somewhat when I so not wish to journey to the chamber before going."

The Sorcery master wizard gestured for us to gather close, and immediately began chanting in a low voice, making complicated hand gestures which were just a bit more dramatic than I thought necessary.

A moment more, and the world flickered around me, and then -- light consumed us.

What seemed like a lifetime later, and I was looking around in another mossless chamber, similar and yet different from the other one. Thankfully, this time my stomach decided not to dance a jig.

In the distance, I heard not the silence of the deep Underdark, but a babble of voices and footsteps.

"Well," the wizard who had transported us said. "We're here."

Part Twenty

We walked forward towards the noise of the city for some minutes in a muted silence. Now what? was the question in the air. We were here, and what did we do. The drow wizards were going to refresh, catch some Z's, banish the Limbo cutters, and go back to their city? Wizard work never comes cheap, and I'm no clueless berk to think that this would be any different. They were after something, and there's no avoiding that. But apparently, they were going to try. Silence was our language in those moments. Uneasy, roiling, and nervous. Damn it, but I wished we could just get it all over with! Unfortunately, it's never that simple.

"I know a little inn here that isn't too rife with plots and thieves," Jalynfein said suddenly, "It will do for our stay."

"Of course, this is Skullport," added the Sorcery Master--and I was just dying to know his name. I mean, at least when you know a name, you know something!--"So no place will be pure."

It's not as though I was expecting purity, you'd have to be a leatherhead to think you'd get that, but weariness settled right in bones at that statement. All I wanted to do was sleep, sleep, and sleep some more! Preferably for about a week straight. But my first glimpse of the city of Skullport completely dispelled that notion. It looked, felt, and smelt sleazy. Oh sure, it had the mysterious aura part down pat, but it was still sleazy. There was no way in Baator that I was going to settle down and nap like a baby here. Light guarded sleeps, sure. Week long hibernation's? No.

We wove through the beings that populated the place in a long, narrow line. By no means were we a happy go lucky adventuring party, off to save the universe single handedly. We were, however, a cobbled together group of people who were likely to turn on each other at an instant's notice. Maybe Zaknafein and Jalynfein had encountered each other in the past, but they also did not seem to be the best of friends either. Wizards as strange, they tend to pick up fancies. Maybe the mage just made the drow mercenary a project or something. I don't know, but I do no that there was *not* complete trust between them. So, there was not complete trust between any of us.

When we reached the "little inn" in which we were to stay, I understood why there wouldn't be a great deal of plotting and thieves here. Mainly, because it was too unpleasant to plot in, and there was nothing to steal. The place was a broken down, one story, converted apartment containing no more than five rooms. In inn keeper was a grinning little dwarf who tried to swindle us for every penny we had. Not that it worked, but still. . .

Finally, though, we were ensconced in our rooms. I shared mine with Freqtw and Zaknafein, and Jalynfein and the Sorcery berk shared another. I carried some jink with me, but none that would do the job on this plane, same for Fretqw and Zaknafein. The drow wizards just hadn't brought much money, so we were stuck in close quarters.

The others were no doubt planning to do something about the Limbodians, who would catch up sooner than later, but I was concerned about one thing. Sleep.

*Only a little,* my was last thought before dropping off.

Famous last words.
